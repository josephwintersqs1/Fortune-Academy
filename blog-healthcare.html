HIPAA Essentials

Healthcare
Fortune Academy November 7, 2025

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) establishes national standards for protecting sensitive patient health information. Whether you're a healthcare provider, administrator, or support staff member, understanding HIPAA requirements is essential for compliance and protecting patient privacy.

What HIPAA Covers: The Foundation of Patient Privacy

Enacted in 1996 and significantly expanded in 2013 through the HITECH Act and Omnibus Rule, HIPAA creates a comprehensive framework for safeguarding protected health information (PHI). The regulations apply to covered entities and their business associates, establishing strict requirements for how patient information can be used, disclosed, and protected.

Protected Health Information (PHI)

PHI includes any individually identifiable health information held or transmitted by a covered entity or business associate in any form—electronic, paper, or oral. This encompasses:

  • Patient names, addresses, and contact information
  • Social Security numbers and medical record numbers
  • Health insurance information and account numbers
  • Diagnoses, treatment information, and test results
  • Billing and payment records
  • Photos, x-rays, and other medical images
  • Any other health information that could identify a patient

The scope is intentionally broad to ensure comprehensive protection. Even information that seems innocuous—like appointment dates or the fact that someone visited a particular clinic—qualifies as PHI when linked to an identifiable individual.

Who Must Comply: Covered Entities and Business Associates

HIPAA compliance obligations extend beyond traditional healthcare providers. Understanding whether your organization falls under HIPAA jurisdiction is the first step toward compliance.

Covered Entities

Three categories of organizations are considered covered entities under HIPAA:

Healthcare Providers: Any provider who transmits health information electronically in connection with covered transactions. This includes physicians, dentists, psychologists, chiropractors, nursing homes, pharmacies, and medical equipment suppliers.

Health Plans: Organizations that provide or pay for medical care, including health insurance companies, HMOs, Medicare, Medicaid, and employer-sponsored health plans.

Healthcare Clearinghouses: Entities that process health information from another entity from a nonstandard format into a standard format, or vice versa.

Business Associates

Business associates are individuals or organizations that perform functions or activities on behalf of covered entities that involve the use or disclosure of PHI. Common examples include:

  • Medical billing companies and claims processors
  • Practice management software vendors
  • Consultants and attorneys who access PHI
  • Transcription services
  • Shredding companies and record storage facilities
  • Email hosting and cloud storage providers handling PHI

The 2013 Omnibus Rule made business associates directly liable for HIPAA violations. If your organization handles PHI on behalf of healthcare providers, you must implement your own compliance program—you can't rely on the covered entity's safeguards alone.

Key HIPAA Rules: Privacy, Security, and Breach Notification

HIPAA consists of several rules that work together to protect patient information. Understanding each component helps organizations build comprehensive compliance programs.

The Privacy Rule

The Privacy Rule establishes standards for how PHI can be used and disclosed. It requires covered entities to:

  • Provide patients with a Notice of Privacy Practices explaining their rights
  • Obtain patient authorization before using or disclosing PHI for most purposes
  • Implement policies and procedures to limit PHI use to the minimum necessary
  • Train workforce members on privacy policies and procedures
  • Designate a Privacy Officer responsible for compliance
  • Implement a complaint process and document complaints

The Security Rule

The Security Rule specifically addresses electronic PHI (ePHI), requiring covered entities and business associates to implement administrative, physical, and technical safeguards:

Administrative Safeguards: Policies and procedures for managing the selection, development, implementation, and maintenance of security measures. This includes risk assessments, workforce training, security incident procedures, and contingency planning.

Physical Safeguards: Physical measures to protect electronic systems and related buildings from natural and environmental hazards and unauthorized intrusion. Examples include facility access controls, workstation security, and device and media controls.

Technical Safeguards: Technology and policies that protect ePHI and control access to it. This includes access controls, audit controls, integrity controls, and transmission security.

The Breach Notification Rule

When unauthorized access, use, or disclosure of PHI occurs, covered entities must:

  • Notify affected individuals within 60 days
  • Report breaches affecting 500+ people to HHS and media outlets
  • Notify HHS annually of breaches affecting fewer than 500 people
  • Document the breach investigation and response

Who Enforces HIPAA: The Office for Civil Rights

The Department of Health and Human Services' Office for Civil Rights (OCR) enforces HIPAA compliance through investigations, audits, and penalties. OCR investigates complaints filed by individuals or organizations and conducts proactive compliance reviews.

HIPAA Violation Tiers and Penalties

Penalties vary based on the level of culpability and can range from $100 to $50,000 per violation, with annual maximums reaching $1.5 million per violation category:

  • Tier 1: Individual did not know and could not have known about the violation ($100-$50,000 per violation)
  • Tier 2: Violation due to reasonable cause, not willful neglect ($1,000-$50,000 per violation)
  • Tier 3: Violation due to willful neglect that was corrected ($10,000-$50,000 per violation)
  • Tier 4: Violation due to willful neglect that was not corrected ($50,000 per violation)

Beyond financial penalties, HIPAA violations can result in criminal charges with prison sentences for certain egregious violations, particularly those involving theft or disclosure of PHI for personal gain.

Common HIPAA Compliance Gaps

Most HIPAA violations aren't intentional. They result from inadequate training, outdated policies, or failure to recognize compliance obligations. Here are the most common gaps Fortune Academy sees in healthcare organizations:

1. Insufficient Risk Analysis

The Security Rule requires regular risk assessments identifying threats to ePHI, but many organizations skip this critical step or conduct superficial analyses. Without thorough risk assessment, you can't implement appropriate safeguards or prioritize security investments.

2. Inadequate Business Associate Agreements

Many healthcare organizations work with vendors who access PHI without executing proper Business Associate Agreements (BAAs). Every vendor relationship involving PHI must be documented with a compliant BAA outlining each party's responsibilities.

3. Weak Access Controls

Allowing employees to access all patient records regardless of job requirements violates the minimum necessary standard. Implement role-based access controls ensuring staff can only access PHI necessary for their specific job functions.

4. Inconsistent Training

HIPAA requires training all workforce members on privacy and security policies, yet many organizations provide only initial onboarding training. Employees need regular refresher training and immediate updates when policies change or breaches occur.

5. Poor Incident Response

When potential breaches occur, delayed or inadequate response can convert minor incidents into major violations. Organizations need clear incident response plans, designated response teams, and regular drills to ensure readiness.

Building a Strong HIPAA Compliance Program

Effective HIPAA compliance requires ongoing commitment, not one-time training. Successful organizations integrate privacy and security into their culture through:

  • Leadership Commitment: Executives must champion privacy and security, providing resources and holding everyone accountable
  • Comprehensive Policies: Written procedures addressing all HIPAA requirements, regularly reviewed and updated
  • Regular Training: Annual training for all staff plus role-specific training for those with elevated access or responsibilities
  • Technical Safeguards: Encryption, secure authentication, audit logs, and regular security testing
  • Vendor Management: Careful selection and oversight of business associates with proper agreements in place
  • Continuous Monitoring: Regular audits, risk assessments, and policy reviews to identify and address gaps proactively
