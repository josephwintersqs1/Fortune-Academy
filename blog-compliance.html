HR Compliance Checklist 2025

HR Compliance
Fortune Academy November 7, 2025

Employment law compliance protects your organization from costly lawsuits, penalties, and reputational damage. This comprehensive checklist covers the essential policies, training programs, and recordkeeping practices every HR professional needs to maintain compliant operations in 2025.

Federal Employment Law Requirements

Understanding federal employment laws forms the foundation of HR compliance. These statutes apply to most employers across all states, though specific requirements vary based on company size and industry.

Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA)

The FLSA establishes minimum wage, overtime pay, recordkeeping, and child labor standards. Key compliance requirements include:

  • Properly classifying employees as exempt or non-exempt
  • Paying at least federal minimum wage (currently $7.25/hour, though many states mandate higher rates)
  • Compensating non-exempt employees at 1.5x their regular rate for hours worked over 40 per week
  • Maintaining accurate time and payroll records for at least three years
  • Posting required workplace notices about employee rights

Common Pitfall: Misclassifying employees as exempt to avoid overtime payments is one of the most frequently litigated employment issues. The Department of Labor scrutinizes these classifications, and misclassification can result in back pay, penalties, and attorney fees.

Title VII of the Civil Rights Act

Title VII prohibits employment discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, and national origin. Employers with 15 or more employees must:

  • Implement anti-discrimination policies and training
  • Establish clear complaint procedures
  • Conduct prompt, thorough investigations of discrimination allegations
  • Take appropriate remedial action when discrimination is found
  • Prohibit retaliation against employees who report discrimination

Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)

The ADA requires employers to provide reasonable accommodations to qualified individuals with disabilities. Essential compliance practices include:

  • Engaging in the interactive process when employees request accommodations
  • Avoiding disability-related questions during interviews
  • Conducting medical examinations only after conditional job offers
  • Maintaining confidentiality of medical information
  • Training managers on proper accommodation procedures

Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA)

Employers with 50 or more employees must provide eligible workers up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave for specified family and medical reasons. Compliance requirements include:

  • Posting FMLA notices and including policy in employee handbooks
  • Tracking employee eligibility (1,250 hours worked in 12 months, employed for 12+ months)
  • Providing required notices within specified timeframes
  • Maintaining health benefits during leave
  • Restoring employees to same or equivalent positions upon return
  • Prohibiting retaliation against employees who take FMLA leave

The Department of Labor recovered nearly $30 million in FMLA violations in 2024 alone. Most violations stem from improper notice, failure to maintain health benefits, or retaliation—all preventable through proper training and processes.

State Employment Law Obligations

State laws often provide greater protections than federal law, creating additional compliance obligations for multi-state employers. Key areas where state laws frequently exceed federal requirements include:

Minimum Wage and Overtime

Many states mandate minimum wages significantly higher than the federal rate. In 2025, states like California, Washington, and Massachusetts require $15+ per hour. Some cities impose even higher minimum wages. Always apply the highest applicable rate—federal, state, or local.

Paid Leave

An increasing number of states require employers to provide paid sick leave, family leave, or both. Requirements vary significantly by state regarding:

  • Accrual rates and annual maximums
  • Permissible reasons for use
  • Notice requirements before taking leave
  • Whether unused leave must carry over year to year
  • Payout requirements upon termination

Salary History Bans

More than a dozen states prohibit employers from asking applicants about salary history, aiming to reduce pay inequity. Employers must train recruiters and hiring managers to avoid these questions and remove them from applications.

Criminal Background Check Limitations

"Ban the box" laws restrict when employers can inquire about criminal history, typically prohibiting questions on initial applications. Some jurisdictions also limit how employers can use criminal history in making employment decisions.

Essential HR Policies for 2025

Comprehensive, clearly written policies set expectations, protect against legal claims, and provide frameworks for consistent decision-making. Every organization should maintain current policies covering:

Equal Employment Opportunity and Anti-Discrimination

Your EEO policy should affirm commitment to providing equal opportunities regardless of protected characteristics. Include your complaint procedure and anti-retaliation provisions. Update policies to address emerging issues like gender identity and sexual orientation discrimination.

Anti-Harassment and Sexual Harassment

Separate anti-harassment policies demonstrate commitment to maintaining respectful workplaces. Clearly define prohibited conduct, outline multiple reporting channels, guarantee thorough investigations, and prohibit retaliation. Many states now mandate specific policy elements and annual training.

Attendance and Leave

Clearly communicate expectations regarding attendance, tardiness, and available leave options including vacation, sick leave, FMLA, disability leave, and other applicable leave types. Specify accrual rates, eligibility requirements, request procedures, and consequences of policy violations.

Compensation and Hours of Work

Explain your pay periods, overtime policies, timekeeping requirements, and meal/rest break entitlements. Address how employees should record all hours worked and report any timekeeping discrepancies.

Social Media and Technology Use

Balance the employer's legitimate business interests with employees' rights to engage in protected concerted activity. Avoid overly broad restrictions that could be deemed to interfere with employees' Section 7 rights under the National Labor Relations Act.

Required Training Programs

Training serves dual purposes: preventing workplace problems and demonstrating good faith compliance efforts that can limit liability if issues arise. Priority training programs include:

Anti-Harassment Training

Many states now mandate harassment prevention training. California requires two hours of training for supervisors and one hour for all employees every two years. New York requires annual training. Connecticut, Delaware, Maine, and Illinois have similar requirements. Even where not legally required, annual training represents best practice.

Supervisor Training

Supervisors need enhanced training covering:

  • Conducting lawful interviews and making hiring decisions
  • Performance management and discipline
  • Recognizing and responding to accommodation requests
  • Identifying and reporting harassment and discrimination
  • Proper leave administration
  • Confidentiality obligations

Workplace Safety Training

OSHA requires employers to provide safety training in languages employees understand. Training should cover hazards specific to employees' roles, proper equipment use, emergency procedures, and injury reporting requirements.

Recordkeeping: What to Maintain and For How Long

Proper recordkeeping protects against compliance claims, supports employment decisions, and satisfies regulatory requirements. Essential records and retention periods include:

Payroll Records (3 years minimum)

  • Employee names, addresses, and Social Security numbers
  • Hours worked each day and week
  • Wage rates and total compensation
  • Deductions and benefit contributions
  • Date of payment and pay periods covered

Employment Records (3-4 years)

  • Applications and resumes (1 year minimum, longer for hired employees)
  • Job descriptions and advertisements
  • Performance evaluations
  • Disciplinary actions
  • Termination documentation

Leave Records (3 years)

  • FMLA certifications and correspondence
  • Accommodation requests and interactive process documentation
  • Workers' compensation claims
  • Leave requests and approvals

Training Records (Duration of employment + 1 year)

  • Harassment prevention training certificates
  • Safety training documentation
  • Policy acknowledgments

Best Practice: Maintain separate personnel files for medical information, I-9 forms, and payroll records. This ensures proper confidentiality and simplifies government audits.

Annual Compliance Calendar

Create a compliance calendar tracking recurring obligations such as:

  • January: File previous year's EEO-1 Report (due March 31), verify I-9 forms, update required workplace posters
  • Quarterly: Review and update job descriptions, audit exempt classifications, review complaint logs
  • Annually: Conduct harassment prevention training, review and update handbook, perform pay equity audits, conduct accessibility reviews
  • As Needed: Post required notices for new laws, update policies for legal changes, train on new requirements
