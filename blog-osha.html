How to Become OSHA Certified

OSHA Safety
Fortune Academy November 7, 2025

OSHA certification protects workers, reduces workplace injuries, and helps companies avoid costly violations. Whether you're a worker seeking safety training or a supervisor responsible for compliance, this guide explains the certification options, requirements, and process for obtaining OSHA credentials.

Understanding OSHA Certification Options

When people talk about "OSHA certification," they're typically referring to one of two training programs: the 10-Hour OSHA Card or the 30-Hour OSHA Card. These programs are designed by the Occupational Safety and Health Administration to provide workers and supervisors with baseline safety knowledge for their industry.

It's important to understand that OSHA certifications are not licenses. They don't expire in the traditional sense, though employers may require periodic refresher training. The cards serve as documentation that you've completed approved safety training meeting OSHA standards.

10-Hour OSHA Training: Entry-Level Safety Certification

The 10-Hour OSHA course is designed for entry-level workers in construction or general industry. This program provides baseline safety knowledge and awareness of common workplace hazards.

Who Should Take the 10-Hour Course?

  • New workers entering construction or industrial environments
  • Workers who need basic OSHA safety training for job requirements
  • Employees in states or industries where 10-hour training is mandated
  • Anyone seeking foundational workplace safety knowledge

What the 10-Hour Course Covers

The curriculum varies slightly between construction and general industry versions, but core topics include:

  • Introduction to OSHA: Worker rights, employer responsibilities, and how to file complaints
  • Hazard Recognition: Identifying common workplace dangers in your industry
  • Fall Protection: Leading cause of construction fatalities and prevention strategies
  • Electrical Safety: Understanding electrical hazards and safe work practices
  • Personal Protective Equipment (PPE): Proper selection, use, and maintenance
  • Health Hazards: Chemical exposures, noise, temperature extremes
  • Emergency Response: Evacuation procedures and emergency action plans

The 10-hour course can be completed in 2-3 days through classroom instruction or at your own pace through Fortune Academy's online platform. Upon completion, you'll receive an official OSHA 10-Hour card issued by the U.S. Department of Labor.

30-Hour OSHA Training: Supervisory-Level Certification

The 30-Hour OSHA course provides more comprehensive coverage of safety topics and is designed for supervisors, foremen, and safety professionals who need in-depth knowledge of OSHA regulations and hazard prevention.

Who Should Take the 30-Hour Course?

  • Supervisors and foremen responsible for worker safety
  • Safety coordinators and managers
  • Workers seeking advanced safety knowledge for career advancement
  • Professionals in high-hazard industries or job sites

The 30-hour certification demonstrates commitment to safety excellence and often qualifies you for safety coordinator roles or promotions into supervisory positions. It's an investment that pays dividends throughout your career.

What the 30-Hour Course Covers

The 30-hour program includes all topics from the 10-hour course plus expanded coverage of:

  • OSHA standards interpretation and compliance requirements
  • Conducting safety inspections and hazard assessments
  • Developing and implementing safety programs
  • Incident investigation and root cause analysis
  • Excavation and trenching safety (construction)
  • Scaffolding safety and fall protection systems
  • Machine guarding and lockout/tagout procedures (general industry)
  • Confined space entry requirements
  • Hazard communication and chemical safety programs

The 30-hour course typically takes 4-5 days in classroom format or can be completed over several weeks through online self-paced learning. Fortune Academy offers both construction and general industry versions with experienced instructors who bring real-world safety management experience.

Construction vs. General Industry: Choosing the Right Course

Both 10-hour and 30-hour courses are offered in two versions: construction and general industry. Choosing the right version depends on your work environment.

Construction OSHA Training

Choose construction training if you work in building construction, highway construction, demolition, or any activity involving construction, alteration, or repair. Construction courses emphasize fall hazards, scaffolding, excavation safety, and heavy equipment operation.

General Industry OSHA Training

Choose general industry training if you work in manufacturing, warehousing, healthcare, retail, or any non-construction work environment. General industry courses focus on machine safety, walking-working surfaces, electrical safety, and hazard communication.

State and Local Requirements: Where OSHA Training Is Mandatory

While OSHA training isn't universally required by federal law, many states and municipalities have made it mandatory for construction workers and supervisors:

  • New York: All workers on public works projects must have 10-hour cards; supervisors need 30-hour
  • Massachusetts: 10-hour required for construction workers on public projects
  • Connecticut: All construction workers must complete 10-hour training
  • Missouri: Required for most public works construction projects
  • Nevada: Required for workers on public works projects over $250,000

Beyond state mandates, many contractors, project owners, and general contractors require OSHA training as a condition of site access. Even if not legally required in your area, having your OSHA card can make you more employable and demonstrate safety commitment to potential employers.

The Certification Process: How to Get Your OSHA Card

Obtaining your OSHA certification is straightforward when you follow these steps:

1. Choose Your Course Format

Fortune Academy offers multiple learning formats to fit your schedule:

  • Online Self-Paced: Complete training on your schedule from any device
  • Live Virtual: Real-time instruction with an OSHA-authorized trainer via video conference
  • In-Person Classroom: Traditional classroom experience at one of our locations

2. Complete the Required Hours

OSHA mandates specific minimum training times. The 10-hour course must be completed over at least two days, and the 30-hour course over at least four days. These minimums ensure adequate time for learning and prevent rushing through critical safety concepts.

3. Pass the Final Assessment

You'll complete quizzes throughout the training and a final exam covering all course material. The assessment ensures you've understood key safety concepts and can apply them in your workplace.

4. Receive Your Card

After successfully completing the course and assessment, Fortune Academy processes your information with OSHA. Within 3-4 weeks, you'll receive your official OSHA card in the mail. Many students also receive a temporary certificate of completion immediately upon finishing the course, allowing them to begin work while awaiting their permanent card.

Refresher Training and Ongoing Safety Education

While OSHA cards don't technically expire, many employers require periodic refresher training—typically every 3-5 years. Refresher courses update you on new regulations, reinforce critical safety practices, and often cover recent accidents or lessons learned in your industry.

Beyond basic OSHA certification, consider specialized safety training in areas like:

  • Competent Person training for scaffolding, fall protection, or excavation
  • Forklift operator certification
  • First aid and CPR
  • Confined space entry
  • Hazardous materials handling

These specialized credentials complement your OSHA training and can qualify you for higher-paying positions or safety coordinator roles.
